New York City, Long Island, Rockland County, and Westchester County were all built by Robert Moses (December 18, 1888 – July 29, 1981), who was known as the “master builder” of the mid-20th century. For his work as an urban planner in the United States, he has been likened to Baron Haussmann of Second Empire Paris on many occasions. He was also probably one of the most divisive characters in the history of urban planning in the United States. 

His choices in favour of roads over public transportation contributed to the development of the contemporary suburbs of Long Island and inspired a generation of engineers, architects, and urban planners who propagated his ideas across the United States and beyond. One of his most significant contributions to urban planning was the creation of New York’s extensive parkway system. Despite the fact that Moses was never elected to any public office (his only attempt at public office was when he ran for governor of New York as a Republican in 1934 and was defeated by a significant margin), he was responsible for the establishment and leadership of numerous public authorities that provided him with considerable autonomy from the general public and elected representatives.

 As a result of Moses’ efforts, New York State has a higher proportion of public benefit corporations than any other US state. As a result, public benefit corporations have become the primary mode of infrastructure construction and maintenance in New York, accounting for 90 percent of the state’s debt. With his position as head of various authorities, he had control over millions of dollars in income generated by his projects’ revenue generation, such as tolls, and he had the authority to issue bonds to borrow vast sums of money, allowing him to embark on new ventures with little or no involvement from legislative bodies.
